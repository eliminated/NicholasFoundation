Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about TNF, our platforms, and working with us.

General

The Nicholas Foundation (TNF) is a technology-first nonprofit foundation dedicated to building open-source systems that drive global impact. Founded in 2015, we develop and maintain platforms in AI, blockchain, connectivity, cybersecurity, and research — deploying them freely to communities, governments, and organizations worldwide.
Yes. The Nicholas Foundation is a registered 501(c)(3) nonprofit organization. We are funded through philanthropic grants, foundation partnerships, enterprise support contracts, and community donations. All surplus funds are reinvested directly into our platforms and research programs.
TNF receives funding from: (1) philanthropic grants from major foundations, (2) enterprise support agreements with organizations using our platforms, (3) government contracting for humanitarian deployments, and (4) individual and organizational donations. Our annual financial report is publicly available for full transparency.
TNF is a fully distributed organization with team members in 28 countries. We have legal registration in the United States and operational hubs coordinated across UTC-8 to UTC+8 time zones. We believe that distributed teams build better global products.

Partnerships

Submit a partnership inquiry via our Contact page. We onboard NGOs, governments, research institutions, and enterprises. The process involves a brief alignment call, technical assessment, and agreement on a partnership scope. Most partnerships are activated within 2-4 weeks.
All TNF platforms are 100% free for NGOs and humanitarian organizations with valid nonprofit registration. Priority support, elevated API limits, and dedicated engineering time are available through our Nonprofit Support tier — also at no cost to qualifying organizations. Enterprise support contracts are available for commercial entities.

Licensing

Most TNF software is released under the MIT License, allowing free commercial and non-commercial use. Some datasets in OpenData Commons use Creative Commons (CC BY 4.0). Protocol specifications like DID.chain are released as open standards with no licensing restrictions. All license details are prominently listed in each project's GitHub repository.
